## Context

Talk by Prof. Paul Wennberg (Caltech). Primary references: Liu et al.,
*AGU Advances* (2020) and Liu et al. (2026, *Geophysical Research Letters*,
in review).

**Central question**: are the high-latitude northern forests a major
contributor to the anthropogenic CO₂ sink?

## The land carbon sink

Over the past 50 years, land and ocean together have absorbed more than
half of all fossil-fuel carbon added to the atmosphere [^sellers2018]. The
residual between the atmospheric growth rate and the ocean sink — derived
algebraically — gives the land sink.

Global budget (approximate, Pg C yr⁻¹):

| Source / sink | Pg C yr⁻¹ |
|---|---|
| Fossil fuel emissions | +9.6 |
| Tropical land (net) | −0.7 |
| Extra-tropical land | −3.0 |

The latitudinal gradient of atmospheric CO₂, fed into inverse models,
points the bulk of extra-tropical uptake toward **high-latitude forests**.
These forests have long sunlit days and are not water-limited — temperature
is the main constraint.

## Observational evidence

### OCO-2 and the Hovmöller diagram

The Orbiting Carbon Observatory-2 (OCO-2) satellite measures column-averaged
CO₂ dry-air mole fraction (XCO₂). Displayed as a zonal-mean Hovmöller diagram:
CO₂ is drawn down at high latitudes each growing season, then the anomaly
propagates equatorward. The signal is large in the Northern Hemisphere and
nearly absent in the Southern Hemisphere — consistent with the absence of a
large austral boreal forest.

### Solar-Induced Chlorophyll Fluorescence

SIF arises from the absorption of sunlight by chlorophyll in the
photosystem. Crucially, it is not merely a proxy for green cover: it tracks
**photosynthetic activity**. A leaf under afternoon heat stress fluoresces
less than the same leaf in the morning.

Key observational result: **SIF and Gross Primary Production (GPP) are
strongly correlated with temperature in the Boreal** — no surprise, since
cold temperatures preclude leaf-out. The empirical relationship is:

$$\mathrm{GPP} = 3.444 \; e^{(0.24 \pm 0.02)\, T}$$

GPP roughly doubles every 3 °C increase in growing-season mean temperature
(GSMT). About 50–60 % of the observed increase is attributable to
**greening** — measured as increased fraction of Photosynthetically Active
Radiation absorbed by the canopy (fPAR) — the rest is increased
light-use efficiency.

Because CO₂ concentration is nearly homogeneous at the spatial scales
considered, the spatial correlation of GPP with temperature effectively
isolates the climate forcing from CO₂ fertilisation.

## Model–observation discrepancy

**The Community Land Model (CLM) and other Earth System Models (ESMs) show
essentially no correlation between GPP and temperature in the Boreal** — a
direct contradiction of the observational record. Within the TRENDY
(Trends in the Land Carbon Cycle) ensemble, models with high temperature
sensitivity tend to have low CO₂ sensitivity and vice versa.
Wennberg's reading of the evidence: temperature sensitivity is the dominant
driver for high-latitude forests, and current ESMs are missing it.

## Seasonal breakdown

Spring phenology dominates the annual signal:

- Across all ecosystem types (evergreen needleleaf, deciduous needleleaf,
  mixed), there is a strong positive response of the SIF anomaly to spring
  temperature anomaly: a warm spring means substantially higher carbon uptake.
- The relationship weakens in summer and disappears in autumn.
- In autumn, productivity is actually **anticorrelated** with temperature
  anomaly: warmer years tend to be drier, suppressing late-season uptake.
- Spring still dominates the annual budget because it coincides with the
  highest photon availability of the year.

## Substituting space for time

The spatial gradient in fPAR across temperature zones can be used to
predict the temporal response to warming — a space-for-time substitution.
This approach successfully reproduces the 25-year trend observed in
Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) greenness data.

## Implications

Under current emissions trajectories, GSMT in high-latitude regions is
projected to rise by 2–3 °C. Given the exponential GPP–temperature
relationship above, **the productivity of boreal forests could double**.

Three degrees of warming is also approximately the temperature difference
between evergreen-dominated and deciduous-dominated forest regimes, implying
a potential biome shift on top of the productivity increase.

Wennberg's summary:

- Net ecosystem exchange in boreal forests is thermally limited.
- Spatial and seasonal GPP patterns are strongly correlated with temperature.
- The correlation is driven by both greening (increased canopy absorption)
  and increased light-use efficiency.
- Current ESMs substantially underestimate this temperature sensitivity.
